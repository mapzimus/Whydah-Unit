Overview

Driving question

"Why would a person choose piracy in 1717 — and how would they want you to tell their story?"

Four supporting questions (one per week)

  1. How do people use maps and navigation to exercise power — in 1717 and today?
  2. What do Atlantic trade routes tell us about who benefits and who suffers from global commerce, then and now?
  3. How does geography shape the choices people can make — from 18th-century piracy to modern chokepoints like the Strait of Hormuz?
  4. Whose stories get mapped, and whose get buried — across time?

Three threads running through the whole unit

  1. Cartography and navigation. Maps are the tool. We learn to read and make them first. The Whydah story holds together on cartography too: Cyprian Southack's 1717 salvage map is what Barry Clifford used in 1984 to find the wreck.
  2. The Whydah's Atlantic world. The ship's journey carries everything else: trade, slavery, piracy, the wreck, the recovery.
  3. Modern Oceans. Hormuz, Suez, Bab-el-Mandeb, Panama, container shipping, the Houthi attacks. Same chokepoint logic as 1717, different water. (Hilary's ask, made structural.)
  4. Running through all three: a social justice lens - whose stories get told, whose get buried. Not a separate pillar; the constant theme across cartography, the Atlantic world, and modern oceans.

How we're holding the moral complexity

The Whydah's crew were escaping real brutality and they stole for a living. The Ship's Articles were democratic and they were a mutiny. A member of Parliament funded the ship as a slaver. We want students to understand these choices without glorifying or condemning them — no flattening either direction.

How the Journal and the capstone work together

The Voyage Journal is a running binder — physical or digital, student choice — that grows one entry per session. Every entry is small and finishable in a single class period: a map, a short reflection, a T-chart, an analysis card. The goal is to build up raw material across the whole unit.

The capstone is a new piece in a format the student picks from the menu below, built from the Journal. Every capstone has to cite at least 3–4 Journal entries as source material. The Journal is the evidence of process; the capstone is the synthesis they present.

At the Showcase, capstones get presented; Journals lay open on tables for visitors to flip through.

Timeline — the Whydah's story

The whole story, start to finish. If we can tell this cold, we can teach it. Tiers mark what we trust, what's disputed, and what's pirate mythology.

Background (pre-Whydah)
Feb 23, 1689
Sam Bellamy born — Hittisleigh, Devonshire, England Solid
Youngest of six; mother Elizabeth died in childbirth three weeks before his baptism on March 18.
1701
John Julian born Solid
Half-Miskito Indian from the Mosquito Coast (modern Nicaragua/Honduras). Later mislabeled as "Cape Cod Indian"; identification revised by Expedition Whydah research.
1704
French establish fortified trading post at Ouidah Solid
European slave-trading infrastructure consolidates at what becomes the second-largest Atlantic slave port.
1707
Scilly Isles disaster Solid
A Royal Navy fleet misjudges its east-west position and wrecks on the Scilly Isles (small rocky islands off southwestern England) — over 1,000 dead. Triggers the 1714 Longitude Act and Britain's hunt for a longitude solution. Context for why navigation was genuinely dangerous in the Whydah era.
1713–14
War of Spanish Succession ends Solid
Treaties of Utrecht and Rastatt. Royal Navy demobilization and end of privateering contracts leave thousands of sailors unemployed, concentrated in the Caribbean. Single biggest push factor into Golden Age piracy.
1714
British Longitude Act Solid
Parliament offers £20,000 for a solution to the longitude problem. Harrison begins work ~1730; his H4 wouldn't prove useful until 1761 — 44 years after the Whydah wrecked.
1715
Spanish Treasure Fleet wrecks off Florida Solid
Salvage gold rush draws former privateers, sailors, and opportunists — including Paulsgrave Williams and Sam Bellamy, who partner to try to salvage.
~1715
Benjamin Hornigold founds the "Flying Gang" at Nassau Solid
Former privateer organizes pirate hub in the Bahamas after the Spanish Treasure Fleet salvage. Bellamy and Edward "Blackbeard" Teach both sail under him — shared mentorship.
The Whydah's Life (1715–1717)
1715
Whydah commissioned in London Solid
Built as a slave ship. Commissioned by Sir Humphrey Morice — Member of Parliament AND London's foremost slave merchant. 110 ft, 300 tons, three-masted galley, 18 cannons. Named after the West African slave port of Ouidah.
Late 1715 / early 1716
Bellamy and Williams turn to piracy Solid
After their Spanish Treasure Fleet salvage failed, the partnership redirects to piracy in the Caribbean. Williams had the capital; Bellamy had navigational skill.
1716
Whydah sails slaving voyage under Capt. Lawrence Prince Solid
Ship's bell (recovered 1985) bears "The Whydah Gally 1716." 367 captives loaded in West Africa; 312 survive the Middle Passage to Jamaica.
Summer 1716
Hornigold deposed — Bellamy elected captain Solid
Hornigold's crew votes him out of command because he refuses to attack English ships. Bellamy elected captain in his place; Blackbeard also deposed. Literal pirate democracy at work — the crew could remove the captain.
Nov 9, 1716
John King, age 8–11, joins Bellamy's crew Solid
Bellamy captures the sloop Bonetta (Jamaica → Antigua). King, a passenger traveling with his mother, demands to join the pirates, threatens suicide if restrained, and threatens his own mother. Bellamy allows him to stay.
Feb 28, 1717
Bellamy captures the Whydah Solid
Off the Bahamas in the Windward Passage, after a three-day chase. Source: Boston Vice-Admiralty Court testimony, 1717 (primary). Bellamy arms her with 28 guns, stores 30+ more below as ballast.
Feb–Apr 1717
Bellamy takes 53+ prizes Solid
Two months of prize-taking through the Caribbean and up the Atlantic coast. Known for releasing crews and typically avoiding gratuitous killing (though not without violence).
April 26, 1717 (day)
Bellamy captures the pink Mary Anne of Dublin Solid
Laden with Madeira wine. The last prize. Seven pirates put aboard as prize crew; she sails with the fleet that evening.
April 26, 1717 (night)
Nor'easter wrecks the Whydah at Wellfleet Solid
At Marconi Beach, Wellfleet, Cape Cod. 146 souls aboard; only 2 Whydah crew survive — John Julian (pilot, 16) and Thomas Davis (Welsh carpenter). The Mary Anne also wrecks the same night south of the Whydah; all 10 aboard survive (7 pirate prize crew + 3 original crew). Williams aboard the Marianne survives by accident — he had stopped at Block Island to visit family.
Aftermath (1717–1930s)
May 1717
Cyprian Southack dispatched to salvage Solid
Massachusetts Governor Samuel Shute sends Cyprian Southack (1662–1745), cartographer and naval militia commander, to recover what can be recovered for the Crown. Southack travels from Boston to Provincetown to Eastham via "Jeremiah's Gutter" — a natural canal through Eastham. Finds the wreck capsized; recovers almost nothing. BUT marks the location on his New England chart.
October 18, 1717
Whydah survivors tried in Boston Solid
Vice-Admiralty Court presided by Governor Samuel Shute and Judge Samuel Sewall — one of the 1692 Salem Witch Trial judges who had publicly apologized for his role in 1697. 9 total survivors tried; 6 found guilty, 2 acquitted, 1 (Julian) not tried.
November 15, 1717
Six hanged at Boston Common Solid
Public execution at the Great Elms. The six: John Brown (Jamaica), Thomas Baker (Netherlands), Hendrick Quintor (Netherlands by record, Black of Dutch–African descent per Clifford & Kinkor 2007), Peter Cornelius Hoof (Sweden), John Shaun (France), Simon van der Vorst (New York). The Boston records list nationality, not ethnicity — the crew was more racially diverse than the paperwork preserves. Cotton Mather — another Salem Witch Trials figure — ministered to them, walked with them to the gallows, preached the execution sermon. Published as "Instructions to the Living, from the Condition of the Dead" (1717) — a primary source that survives.
Nov 22, 1718
Blackbeard killed at Ocracoke Solid
Edward Teach, Bellamy's fellow alumnus of Hornigold's Flying Gang, dies in battle against Lt. Robert Maynard's crew off Ocracoke Island, NC — 19 months after Bellamy.
1724
Captain Charles Johnson publishes A General History of the Pyrates Mixed reliability
Source of most pirate lore. Author identity contested (Colin Woodard favors publisher Nathaniel Mist). Contains valuable material AND significant fabrication. Bellamy's "rob the poor" speech likely invention. Most set speeches likewise.
1727
Kingdom of Dahomey conquers Ouidah Solid
West African political upheaval at the port that gave the Whydah her name. Dahomey continues the slave trade for another 135+ years.
1733
John Julian dies Solid
Died in slavery after multiple escape attempts. Sold from John Quincy (grandfather of future President John Quincy Adams) to another owner. 32 years old, 16 years after the wreck.
1934
Elizabeth Reynard publishes The Narrow Land Literary, not primary
Coins the name "Maria Hallett" for Bellamy's Cape Cod lover. Folklore study, not historical documentation. Students will encounter the name; important to know where it actually came from.
Modern Recovery (1984–present)
1984
Barry Clifford finds the wreck Solid
Using Cyprian Southack's 267-year-old map. Buried under 10–50 ft of sand, only 4 m deep. A 1717 cartographer's map found a 1984 discovery — the whole story held together by cartography.
1985
Ship's bell recovered, identity confirmed Solid
Inscription "The Whydah Gally 1716" proves the wreck is the Whydah. First fully authenticated Golden Age pirate shipwreck in North America.
1986
Kenneth Kinkor joins as Project Historian Solid
Compiles Whydah Sourcebook of 17th–18th-century archival records. Establishes ~25%-Black-crew finding. Serves 27 years until his death in 2013.
2006
John King's remains identified Solid
Smithsonian identifies an 11-inch fibula encased in a shoe and silk stocking as belonging to a young boy of King's approximate age. Small silver buckle also attributed to him.
2016
DNA test on a possible Bellamy descendant — no match Solid
A presumed descendant from Devon was tested against recovered remains; the DNA didn't match. Identification of the wreck's individual dead remains an ongoing puzzle.
2018
Possible Bellamy remains found Reported, not confirmed
A femur in a mineral concretion, recovered alongside weapons, a crushed helmet, and an ornate pistol wrapped in ribbon. Announced as a possible Bellamy candidate; identification has not been confirmed.
2021
Six pirate skeletons recovered in concretions Solid
Identification work via modern DNA techniques continues. Each concretion is a slow extraction — bones embedded with corroded metal artifacts.
Ongoing
Excavation continues Solid
200,000+ artifacts recovered to date; the wreck site is not fully excavated. Real Pirates Salem houses much of the traveling collection.

Why piracy — push/pull causal map

The core of the driving question. The push/pull framing comes from historian Marcus Rediker (Villains of All Nations, 2004) and other research on what happened to sailors after the Royal Navy let most of them go in 1714. The critique at the bottom matters — Rediker's argument is strong, but not everyone buys it. We hold it as one read of the evidence, not the only one.

Push Factors — away from alternatives

End of the war (1713–14)
After the war, the Royal Navy fired most of its sailors and stopped hiring privateers (legal pirates who worked for the Crown), leaving thousands of trained sailors unemployed, many stranded in the Caribbean. Single biggest cause of the Golden Age surge.
Merchant-ship conditions
Rotten food, payment fraud, cramped quarters, untreated disease, crippling accidents, arbitrary abuse from captains.
Slave-ship crew mortality: 21.6% per voyage (1784–90 House of Commons survey — 1-in-4 died)
Falling pay
Trade was booming, so there were more sailors than jobs. Merchants used that to pay them less and less.
Royal Navy wages set 1653 — not raised for 144 years (until British sailors mutinied in 1797 at a place called Spithead)
Impressment (forced Navy service)
Forced naval service was common practice — the Royal Navy seized men off streets and merchant ships. Deserting the navy to join pirates was a direct path.
Land taken from English farmers (the "enclosure movement")
When wealthy landowners fenced off shared common land in England, small farmers lost their living. Many of those men ended up on ships because there was nowhere else to go.

Pull Factors — what piracy offered

Democratic governance
Pirates elected their captains, voted on matters of moment, and could depose a captain who fell out of favor. Bellamy's crew literally voted Hornigold out in summer 1716. Radical for the era.
Fair share of the loot
A pirate ship's written rules ("Articles") spelled out shares by rank. The captain typically got only about 1.5–2× an ordinary seaman — versus massive captain-to-crew ratios on merchant and naval ships.
Flatter racial hierarchy
Kinkor's research: ~25% of Bellamy's crew was Black. Golden Age pirate crews were often 25–33% Black overall. Formerly enslaved men could hold skilled positions unavailable elsewhere.
Compensation for injury
Articles commonly specified set payments for lost limbs or eyes — a form of early workers' compensation, centuries ahead of merchant and naval service.
Loosely defended trade
Colonial shipping was rich and poorly protected. Caribbean power vacuum made piracy unusually lucrative and feasible in this window.
The critique we have to keep in mind. Rediker reads pirates as people pushing back against unfair work and a brutal economy. Other historians push back on him. They argue (1) calling pirates "heroes" misses how often they tortured people for cruelty, and (2) framing the multinational crews as a freedom story can flatten the actual experience of enslaved people on those ships. Both critiques are fair. The push/pull framing explains why people became pirates — and the people who chose piracy also chose theft and violence.

And one more anti-romantic fact. Pirates significantly disrupted the Atlantic slave trade in the 1710s–20s — enough that European powers made wiping out piracy a top priority. But the slave trade rebounded: by the 1730s it had doubled compared to the late 1710s/20s peak of piracy. Pirates interrupted; they didn't stop. We hold all of these halves.

Maps, geography & tools

This is where we lean hardest on the GIS side. Weeks 1, 3, and 4 all come out of here — what navigators in 1717 actually had, what didn't exist yet, and why the Atlantic trade was shaped the way it was.

Navigation instruments — what was on the Whydah

Compass centuries old by 1717
Magnetized needle on a pivot. Gave a coarse heading. Standard on all serious sea vessels.
Backstaff invented John Davis, 1594
The standard latitude tool of the 17th–early 18th century. Measured the sun's altitude from its shadow — user didn't look at the sun directly. This is what the Whydah's 16-year-old pilot John Julian would have used.
Quadrant medieval, still used
Heavy metal plate with a plumb-bob. Measured angle to Polaris at night or sun at noon. More awkward than the backstaff but functional.
Dead reckoning method, not instrument
Estimating current position from last known point using heading + speed + elapsed time. Error accumulated over days. For ocean voyages, it was mostly educated guessing.

What DIDN't exist yet in 1717

Octant 1731, fourteen years too late
Hadley''s reflecting octant. A small mirror let you see horizon and sun in the same eyepiece - faster and more accurate than the backstaff.
Sextant 1759
Wider arc than the octant (60 degrees vs 45) so it could measure lunar distances - the workaround for finding longitude before chronometers became affordable.
Marine chronometer (Harrison''s H4) proved useful 1761, 44 years too late
The clock that solved longitude. Lost only ~5 seconds over an 81-day voyage. H5 (pictured) is the production-ready version Harrison built after H4 won the Longitude Prize.

The longitude problem — a 1717 reality check

In 1717, sailors could reliably find their latitude (north-south position). They could NOT reliably find their longitude (east-west position). The best method available was dead reckoning, which accumulated error over days.

1707 Scilly Isles disaster: Royal Navy fleet misjudged longitude, wrecked on the Scillies, over 1,000 dead. Triggered the 1714 Longitude Act — Parliament offered £20,000 (millions today) for a solution.

John Harrison began work on marine chronometers ~1730. His H4 proved itself in 1761, losing only ~5 seconds over an 81-day voyage. All of this is AFTER the Whydah.

S13 leans into this. The Whydah didn't wreck because the crew was bad. They wrecked because the tools to know your east-west position at night didn't exist yet.

Mercator projection — why Google Maps still uses it

Invented by Gerard Mercator in 1569 but NOT widely adopted for marine charts until the 18th century. By 1717, Mercator charts were becoming the working standard for ocean navigation.

The revolutionary feature: a "rhumb line" (a course where you hold the same compass heading the whole way) shows up as a straight line on a Mercator chart. On any other projection it would be a curve.

Practical effect for a 1717 navigator: draw a straight line from A to B on a Mercator chart, read the compass bearing off that line, hold that heading across the ocean.

Today: Google Maps uses Web Mercator. Same projection a 1717 sailor used. Same Greenland-is-huge distortion.

Herman Moll's 1719 world map — the mapmaker who knew pirates

Moll was the most important London cartographer of the early 1700s. His 1719 world map was made to illustrate Daniel Defoe's Farther Adventures of Robinson Crusoe.

His social circle included actual pirates and privateers: William Dampier, Woodes Rogers, and William Hacke, who gave him firsthand information on Caribbean routes and Spanish treasure movements.

Moll's West Indies map has been described as "a guide to English piracy and privateering." His maps showed trade winds, compass variations, and "Remarkable Tracts" of voyages — essentially commercial intelligence.

In Session 3, this answers the question "who made this map, and why?" — a guy who drank with pirates and sold their route data for profit. Maps are arguments, not neutral records.

Atlantic trade winds & gyres — the triangle trade's physics

The triangle trade's shape wasn't a "choice" by merchants — it was forced by physics.

  • North Atlantic Gyre — giant clockwise ocean current system; the Gulf Stream is its northern limb
  • Trade winds (easterlies, south of ~30°N) — carried ships Europe → Africa → Caribbean
  • Westerlies (westerlies, north of ~30°N) — carried ships Caribbean → North America → Europe

The Whydah's final voyage fits this pattern: after the Caribbean, Bellamy was heading north up the American coast — riding the Gulf Stream toward the westerlies — when the nor'easter caught him off Cape Cod.

S5 move: open the wind map before plotting the trade triangle. "Why is it shaped like a triangle?" has a physical answer, not just an economic one.

Projections & coordinates

Where maps and the globe stop agreeing. The cartography spine of the unit needs students to understand why a map is always a translation, never a copy.

Latitude vs longitude - what they are and why one was easy

Latitude lines run east-west; they measure how far north or south you are from the equator. Longitude lines (also called meridians) run north-south; they measure how far east or west you are from a chosen prime meridian (since 1884, that''s Greenwich, England).

Latitude is geometric. Anyone with a sextant or backstaff can measure the sun''s noon altitude or the angle to Polaris and read latitude off a table. Sailors had been doing this competently since the 15th century.

Longitude is a clock problem. The Earth rotates 360 degrees every 24 hours, so 15 degrees of longitude = 1 hour of time difference. To know your longitude, you need to know two times simultaneously: local time (easy - look at the sun) and the time at a reference port (hard - because your reference clock has to keep accurate time on a rolling, salty, temperature-swinging ship for months).

This is why the Whydah era was a longitude-blind era. Latitude sailing was standard. Longitude was dead-reckoning approximation that piled up error every day. The fix - a sea-worthy chronometer - did not exist when the Whydah wrecked.

Why this matters for us: the Whydah grounded at Wellfleet on the night of April 26, 1717, in a nor''easter, in the dark, on a lee shore. They could not verify their east-west position. A 16-year-old pilot (John Julian) was navigating with a backstaff. This video is the fastest way for a student to feel why Session 13 frames the wreck partly as a longitude-problem disaster.

Map projections - why no flat map can be right

You cannot flatten a sphere onto a plane without distorting something. This is mathematically provable - a theorem called Gauss''s Theorema Egregium (1827). Every world map is a choice about which property to preserve and which to sacrifice. The four main properties are:

  • Conformal - preserves local shapes and angles. Mercator is conformal. Useful for navigation. Sacrifices area badly at high latitudes.
  • Equal-area - preserves relative size of regions. Mollweide, Goode homolosine, Equal Earth. Useful for showing distribution (population, vegetation, voting). Sacrifices shape.
  • Equidistant - preserves distances along certain lines (typically from a single point or along meridians). Useful for measuring travel range from a hub.
  • Compromise - preserves nothing exactly but balances all four properties to be visually reasonable. Robinson and Winkel Tripel are compromise projections used by National Geographic and the AGS.

Watch: Why all world maps are wrong (Vox, Johnny Harris)

Six minutes. Hits the Mercator-vs-Africa moment, the 1973 Gall-Peters controversy, and the trade-offs every projection makes. Show this in S2 or S3 alongside the Moll 1719 close-read.

Tissot''s indicatrix - the visual proof

Nicolas Tissot, a French mathematician, proposed a clean test in 1859: place identical circles at regular intervals across the globe, then watch what those circles become on each projection. Where they stay circular, the projection is conformal. Where they grow or shrink, area is being distorted. The picture does the explaining.

Why Mercator survived - and why Google Maps still uses it

Mercator''s 1569 projection was a navigational tool, not a map for general public consumption. Its decisive property is that any straight line drawn on it is a rhumb line - a course of constant compass bearing. A 1717 navigator could draw a straight line from Cape Cod to the Azores, read the bearing off that line, and just hold that compass heading the entire voyage. Curved courses (great circles) are shorter but require constant heading adjustments most period sailors couldn''t make reliably.

Google Maps uses Web Mercator for the same reason a sailor used Mercator: at every zoom level, the projection is locally conformal, so a square block in Brooklyn looks like a square. The price is that Greenland is unreasonably enormous. For the local-zoom use case, that''s an acceptable trade.

  • S4 (My Maps + Adopt a Ship) - Google My Maps is Web Mercator. Same projection, 307 years later.

  • S13 (The Wreck) - the longitude problem made the wreck partly a navigation disaster.

Salem connection

This is Salem's own story, not a distant pirate one. Two direct Salem-to-Whydah links: the triangle trade built our town, and the Witch Trial figures ran the pirate trials.

Salem's own maritime economy

Salem was built on the same trade the Whydah sailed through.

From the mid-17th century onward, Salem merchants provisioned West Indies plantations with dried cod, livestock, and lumber; Salem ships returned with sugar, molasses, rum, and coffee. The National Park Service is explicit that Salem's waterfront economy depended on slavery.

Even after Massachusetts abolished slavery in 1783, Salem merchants continued in the slave economy — Joshua Ward's Brig Favorite sailed to Africa in 1786 to buy and sell people.

The Ward Family Papers at Peabody Essex Museum document this directly.

The Salem Witch Trial figures who ran the Whydah trials

Our students will already know these names.

Cotton Mather — Puritan minister with complicated role in the 1692 Salem Witch Trials. 25 years later, ministered to the Whydah pirates in Boston, preached their execution sermon, and published "Instructions to the Living, from the Condition of the Dead" (1717) — an existing primary source.

Judge Samuel Sewall — One of the 1692 Salem Witch Trial judges. In 1697 published a public apology — the only Witch Trial judge to do so. Presided over the Whydah pirate trials in Boston, October 1717.

The same New England religious and legal establishment that ran the Salem Witch Trials (1692) ran the Whydah pirate trials (1717). For Salem students, this isn't a distant pirate story — it's their town's own figures, 25 years later.

Glossary

Quick definitions for the technical terms used across the dashboard. Type in the search box to filter. Bold term = direct first-use anchor in another tab.

Articles (Ship''s Articles)
The written rules and shares-of-loot agreement that pirate crews signed on joining a ship. Bartholomew Roberts'' 1721 Articles survive (via Johnson 1724) and are the analogue we teach. The Whydah''s own Articles do not survive, but trial testimony confirms the crew had them and the Wounded Heart wax seals are physical evidence of the signing ritual.
Backstaff
Sun-altitude measuring instrument invented by John Davis in 1594. The standard latitude tool of the 17th and early 18th centuries. The user faced away from the sun and read its shadow on a graduated arc - safer than looking at the sun through earlier instruments.
Bathymetry
The measurement of underwater depth and underwater terrain. Modern Google Earth shows seafloor bathymetry; we use it in S13 to read the wreck site.
Cartography
The discipline of making and reading maps. From Greek khartes (map) + graphein (to write). Cyprian Southack and Herman Moll were the cartographers most directly relevant to the Whydah story.
Chokepoint
A narrow body of water through which a large fraction of global shipping must pass. Hormuz, Suez, Bab-el-Mandeb, Malacca, Panama, Gibraltar. Geographic features that give the country controlling them disproportionate leverage. Pirates concentrated near them in 1717 for the same reason modern state actors target them today.
Compass
Magnetized needle on a pivot; gives heading relative to magnetic north. Standard navigation instrument by 1717 - centuries old by then.
Conformal projection
A map projection that preserves local shapes and angles but distorts area. Mercator is the most famous conformal projection. Useful for navigation; not useful for showing relative size of countries.
Dead reckoning
Estimating current position from a last-known point using heading + speed + elapsed time. The only longitude method available to 1717 sailors. Error accumulated daily.
Demobilization
The discharge of military personnel after a war ends. The Royal Navy demobilization after the War of Spanish Succession (1713-14) put thousands of trained sailors out of work, concentrated in the Caribbean - the single biggest push factor into Golden Age piracy.
Driving Question (DQ)
The unit''s overarching question that students keep returning to. Ours: "Why would a person choose piracy in 1717 - and how would they want you to tell their story?"
Equal-area projection
A map projection that preserves the relative areas of regions but distorts shape. Mollweide, Goode homolosine, Equal Earth. Useful for showing distributions (population, vegetation).
Equator
The great circle on Earth equidistant from the North and South Poles. Latitude zero. The reference line for all latitude measurements.
Flying Gang
The pirate community organized at Nassau by Benjamin Hornigold ~1715, after the Spanish Treasure Fleet salvage. Sam Bellamy and Edward "Blackbeard" Teach both sailed under Hornigold here.
Galley
A type of ship combining sails and oars. The Whydah Gally was a galley-class three-masted slaver, 110 ft, 300 tons.
Great circle
The shortest path between two points on a sphere - the line that would result if you sliced the sphere through both points and through its center. Modern airliners fly great circles. Sailors before the chronometer could not reliably follow them.
Gyre
A large system of circular ocean currents. The North Atlantic Gyre - clockwise rotation between North America, Europe, Africa, and the Caribbean - physically forced the shape of the triangle trade.
Impressment
Forced naval service. The Royal Navy seized men off streets and merchant ships into compulsory military service. Deserting impressed naval service was a direct path into piracy.
Latitude
Distance north or south of the equator, measured in degrees (0 at the equator, 90 at the poles). Findable with a sextant or backstaff in 1717.
Lee shore
A coastline that is downwind of a sailing vessel - the wind is pushing the ship toward the shore. The most dangerous configuration for a ship in a storm. The Whydah was on a lee shore at Wellfleet on the night of April 26, 1717.
Longitude
Distance east or west of the prime meridian (Greenwich since 1884), measured in degrees. Not findable with reasonable accuracy at sea until John Harrison''s H4 chronometer proved itself in 1761 - 44 years after the Whydah wrecked.
Mercator projection
Cylindrical conformal map projection introduced by Gerardus Mercator in 1569. Its decisive property: rhumb lines (constant-bearing courses) appear as straight lines, making it ideal for navigation. Distorts area badly at high latitudes. Web Mercator (a variant) is what Google Maps uses.
Middle Passage
The forced sea voyage of enslaved Africans across the Atlantic to the Americas. The Whydah carried 367 captives from West Africa; 312 survived to Jamaica.
Mutiny
Sailors collectively refusing to obey their captain. In the Royal Navy, a capital offense. On a pirate ship, the foundational moment - most pirate crews were mutineers from naval or merchant service.
Nor''easter
A large storm system in the North Atlantic that travels northeastward along the U.S. East Coast, with winds blowing from the northeast. The April 26, 1717 nor''easter wrecked the Whydah at Wellfleet.
Pink (ship type)
A small, narrow-stern Dutch sailing ship used for cargo and fishing in the 17th-18th centuries. The Mary Anne, a pink of Dublin loaded with Madeira wine, was the prize Bellamy took on April 26, 1717 - and lost the same night.
Primary source
A document, artifact, or testimony created at or near the time of the events being studied. Boston Vice-Admiralty Court testimony (1717), Cotton Mather''s execution sermon (1717), and Cyprian Southack''s 1717 chart are primary sources for the Whydah. Johnson''s General History of the Pyrates (1724) is partially primary as an artifact, but unreliable for specifics.
Privateer
A private armed ship licensed by a government to attack enemy shipping in wartime. Legal piracy with paperwork. When the War of Spanish Succession ended in 1713-14, thousands of privateers lost their commissions - many slid into illegal piracy.
Projection (map projection)
A mathematical method for representing the curved surface of the Earth on a flat map. Every projection distorts something - shape, area, distance, or direction - because flattening a sphere is mathematically impossible without trade-offs.
Prime meridian
The line of longitude defined as zero degrees, used as the reference for all longitude measurements. Standardized at Greenwich, England in 1884.
Punonakanit Wampanoag
The Indigenous people whose territory included Wellfleet, Cape Cod - the place the Whydah wrecked. Their voice is absent from the 1717 written record. Land acknowledgment is appropriate when teaching the wreck site.
Quadrant
Heavy metal-plate sun-altitude instrument with a plumb-bob. Older than the backstaff, still in use in 1717. More awkward to use at sea but functional.
Quartermaster
On a pirate ship, an elected officer second in command, responsible for distributing loot and representing the crew''s interests against the captain. Paulsgrave Williams was the Whydah''s quartermaster.
Rhumb line
A course on the Earth''s surface where you hold a constant compass heading the entire way. On a Mercator chart it shows up as a straight line. Not the shortest path between two points (a great circle is) - but the easiest to navigate before precise instruments.
Sextant
Precision sun- and star-altitude instrument introduced 1759. The successor to the backstaff and quadrant. Did not exist when the Whydah sailed.
Sloop
A small single-masted sailing vessel with a fore-and-aft rig. Common in 17th-18th-century coastal trade. Bellamy''s Marianne (10 guns) and the Bonetta were sloops.
Source criticism
Evaluating a historical source for who created it, when, why, and how reliable it is. The whole reason we tier our own claims (Solid, Contested, Mythologized) and explicitly flag Johnson 1724 as unreliable.
Tissot''s indicatrix
A visualization invented by Nicolas Tissot in 1859. Place identical circles at regular intervals across the globe; project them onto each map. The way the circles deform shows what the projection distorts and where. See the Projections & Coordinates tab for a side-by-side.
Trade winds
Steady easterly winds south of about 30 degrees North that blow from Africa toward the Americas. Combined with the westerlies (north of 30 degrees N) that blow from America back toward Europe, they physically force the triangular shape of Atlantic trade.
Triangle trade
The 17th-18th century Atlantic trade pattern: European manufactured goods to West Africa, enslaved people from Africa to the Caribbean and Americas, raw materials (sugar, molasses, rum, cotton, tobacco) from the Americas back to Europe. The Whydah was built for this trade and named after Ouidah, a major West African slave port.
Vice-Admiralty Court
British colonial maritime court that tried piracy and other sea-related offenses. The Whydah survivors were tried at the Boston Vice-Admiralty Court in October 1717, presided over by Governor Samuel Shute and Judge Samuel Sewall (the apologetic 1692 witch-trial judge).
Voyage Journal
The student running portfolio across the unit. One deliverable per session, accumulated in a physical 3-ring binder pre-tabbed by week. The raw material the capstone draws on.
Westerlies
Prevailing winds from the west in the mid-latitudes (roughly 30-60 degrees N and S). Carried 18th-century ships from the Americas back to Europe along the northern leg of the triangle trade.
Whydah Gally
The slave ship built in London 1715, captured by Sam Bellamy in February 1717 in the Caribbean, wrecked off Wellfleet, Cape Cod on April 26, 1717. 110 ft, 300 tons, three-masted galley, 28+ guns when she wrecked. Discovered by Barry Clifford 1984; bell recovered 1985 confirmed identity.

Confidence & methods

What the confidence tiers mean

Solid We trust it — multiple reputable sources or primary-source documented
Contested It's disputed, attribution is shaky, or it's interpretation rather than fact
Mythologized Traces only to Johnson's 1724 General History of the Pyrates or popular retellings — we shouldn't teach it as fact
Where this all came from. Most of the research for this dashboard drew on Wikipedia, museum sites, academic reviews, and secondary summaries. We haven't gone directly to Rediker's Villains of All Nations, Kinkor's Whydah Sourcebook, or Woodard's Republic of Pirates yet — so any claim that hangs on their specific arguments, we want to double-check against the primary text before teaching it.

The research cutoff is May 2025. Anything newer — fresh scholarship, current museum exhibits, recent excavation finds — isn't reflected here and we should chase it down as we go.
